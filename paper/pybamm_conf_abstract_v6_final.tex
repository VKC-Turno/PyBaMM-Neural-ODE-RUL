{\bfseries\large One-hour characterisation to full state-of-health trajectory:\\
a supplier-agnostic PyBaMM-trained neural operator for second-life LFP RUL}\\[0.5em]
\underline{Krishna Chaitanya Vaddepally}\\
\emph{Turno (Blubble Pvt.\ Ltd.), Bengaluru, India}\\
krishna.vaddepally@turno.club
\end{center}
\vspace{0.3em}

Second-life reuse of used lithium iron phosphate (LFP) cells is bottlenecked
by characterisation cost: reliable remaining-useful-life (RUL) estimation
today requires months of cycling data per cell, and generalising across
supplier chemistries typically demands supplier-specific models. We present
a supplier-agnostic neural operator that maps a single one-hour
characterisation snapshot --- open-circuit voltage (OCV), hybrid pulse power
characterisation (HPPC), galvanostatic intermittent titration (GITT) and a
partial reference performance test (RPT) --- to the full state-of-health
(SoH) trajectory to end-of-life. The operator is trained on a
PyBaMM-generated~[1] corpus and validated on held-out cells from three
commercial LFP suppliers (CALB, EVE, REPT). The result is a common
trajectory operator that transfers across chemistries without per-supplier
retraining, closing the gap between physics-based degradation modelling and
the operational latency that second-life screening demands.

Our pipeline runs in three phases. In \textbf{Phase~1} we identify per-cell
beginning-of-life (BOL) electrochemistry from the characterisation
snapshot: OCV--SOC inversion yields the stoichiometric window, GITT the
solid-state diffusivity, HPPC the series resistance, and a partial RPT the
initial capacity. In \textbf{Phase~2} we calibrate PyBaMM's Single-Particle
Model with electrolyte (SPMe) on the Prada2013 LFP chemistry~[2] per anchor
cell via differential evolution against measured long-term cycling,
identifying five degradation parameters ($k_{\text{SEI}}$, $V_{\text{SEI}}$,
$D_{\text{SEI,solvent}}$, $k_{\text{plating}}$, $k_{\text{LAM,neg}}$); the
negative-electrode loss-of-active-material channel is confirmed as the
dominant fade contributor for this chemistry. \textbf{Phase~3} draws Sobol
perturbations in log-encoded $\theta$-space around each of seven Phase-2
anchors under a decorrelation gate, simulates each perturbed trajectory to a
2\,500-cycle horizon under a 0.5C/25\,\textdegree C protocol ($N = 490$
trajectories, 95.1\,\% pass gating, zero solver errors, 4.2\,h wall on a
5-core CPU), and trains a $\theta$-conditioned Neural ODE~[3] with a
\emph{softplus}-monotonic decoder integrated by \texttt{torchdiffeq}
(\texttt{dopri5}). Because 51\,\% of simulated trajectories terminate before
crossing SoH~$= 0.80$ within horizon, we adopt a Tobit-style censored loss
that treats horizon-truncated trajectories as informative lower bounds
rather than discarding them.

The anchor cohort spans nearly the full plausible LFP fade envelope: the
fastest-fade anchor (CALB\_0009) reaches EoL~(SoH~$= 0.80$) at 1\,865
cycles, while the slowest (CALB\_0015) does not cross the EoL threshold
within a 5\,000-cycle simulation horizon. We validate the trained operator
on three held-out cells drawn from all three suppliers --- CALB\_0029,
EVE\_0003, REPT\_0031 --- using a zero-shot nearest-supplier-anchor
$\theta$ prior (no per-cell degradation fit). In-window SoH root-mean-square
errors of 1.05, 1.76 and 1.69 percentage points (mean 1.50\,pp) fall well
inside a 3.0\,pp acceptance threshold set from first principles
(Figure~\ref{fig:heldout}); trajectory Pearson correlation
$r = 0.89, 0.54, 0.80$. Crucially, the standard deviation of per-cell error
across the three suppliers is only 0.32\,pp: with a single
supplier-median $\theta$ prior, prediction accuracy is essentially
chemistry-independent across CALB, EVE and REPT LFP variants.

Two limitations bound the result: a Fisher-cosine identifiability
diagnostic returns $|\cos| \geq 0.96$ between the $k_{\text{SEI}}$ and
$k_{\text{LAM,neg}}$ gradient columns --- accurate trajectories, limited
physical disambiguation --- and the cross-supplier claim rests on seven
anchor cells. Expanding the anchor pool and per-anchor batched training
are the natural next steps.
